Aggregated Review:
### Key Points
This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the Elo rating system under the Bradley-Terry-Luce (BTL) model, utilizing Markov chain theory to demonstrate that the Elo update system converges to true player ratings with high probability. The authors derive convergence guarantees for time-averaged dynamics, showing that the error is bounded by \((e^{4M}/\lambda_q)(n^{-\alpha} + \log(n) \sqrt{1/t})\) in \(\ell_2\)-norm. Additionally, the paper explores efficient tournament designs to enhance the Elo process's convergence, providing rigorous proofs and empirical data to support the findings.

### Strengths and Weaknesses
Strengths:  
- The paper offers a thorough theoretical analysis of the Elo system, contributing significantly to the understanding of its performance under the BTL model.  
- The exploration of tournament design in relation to the fastest-mixing Markov chain problem is a novel contribution, providing practical insights for tournament organizers.  
- The model is well-explained, and the results are presented clearly, with a commendable effort to provide intuition and structure.

Weaknesses:  
- The extensive definition of new variables lacks detailed explanations, making the paper challenging to understand.  
- The convergence rate results are based on strong assumptions, particularly regarding the "burn-in phase," limiting their applicability.  
- The contribution may not be as significant as claimed, as the results appear similar to existing literature, raising questions about the novelty of the techniques used.

### Suggestions for Improvement
We recommend that the authors improve the clarity of variable definitions and provide more detailed explanations to enhance understanding. Additionally, addressing the convergence rate from the start phase, rather than just the burn-in phase, would strengthen the paper. We suggest reordering results in section 2.1 to streamline the presentation and clarify the relationship between Theorems 2.5, 2.7, and 2.8. Lastly, we encourage the authors to explicitly discuss the novelty of their techniques in comparison to existing work to better highlight their contributions.
