Aggregated Review:
### Key Points
This paper presents a new recommendation system framework called MixRec (Individual and Collective Mixing for Data Augmentation), designed to address data sparsity in recommender systems. MixRec employs two data augmentation strategies: individual mixing, which generates positive samples related to the target user or item, and collective mixing, which combines characteristics of users or items within a batch. The authors claim that extensive experiments on four real-world datasets show MixRec outperforms various baseline methods in recommendation performance and training efficiency.

### Strengths and Weaknesses
Strengths:
- The dual-mixing contrastive learning method effectively utilizes newly constructed samples, enhancing positive sample comparisons.
- The proposed method is simple, scalable, and effective, with a clear description of the mixing mechanism.
- The experimental section is detailed, providing a comprehensive list of baselines for comparison.

Weaknesses:
- The novelty of the method is questionable, as it appears similar to LightGCN with only minor modifications to InfoNCE.
- The experimental content lacks robustness analysis, particularly regarding interaction noise.
- Interpretability is poor, with insufficient qualitative analysis to clarify how the method operates.
- The model explanation in Section 2.4 is confusing, with inconsistencies in the use of hard negative samples and the parameter $\beta$.
- The impact may be limited to graph-based recommendation models, lacking effectiveness in other scenarios like sequential recommendation.

### Suggestions for Improvement
We recommend that the authors improve the clarity of the model explanation in Section 2.4 by addressing the inconsistencies regarding hard negative samples and the parameter $\beta$. Additionally, the authors should conduct a robustness analysis to evaluate the method's performance against interaction noise. To enhance interpretability, we suggest including qualitative analyses that elucidate the workings of the proposed method. Furthermore, we recommend that the authors provide detailed hyperparameter settings and training logs for reproducibility, as discrepancies in reported results were noted. Lastly, consider expanding the experimental comparisons beyond graph-based methods to demonstrate broader applicability.
