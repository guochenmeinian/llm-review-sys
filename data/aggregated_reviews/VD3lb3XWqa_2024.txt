Aggregated Review:
### Key Points
This paper presents a test designed to evaluate whether large language models (LLMs) exhibit self-awareness, akin to the Turing test. The test involves the LLM identifying itself among interlocutors in a dialogue, aiming to assess its ability to distinguish its actions from those of others. The authors argue that this self-awareness Turing test is objective and can be conducted from a third-person perspective, requiring the subject to identify outputs that genuinely originate from themselves. The distinction between general and specific instances of the test is crucial, with the general form encompassing all levels of self-awareness. The authors conclude that current LLMs do not demonstrate self-awareness based on their performance in this task and acknowledge that future rigorous studies could enhance understanding but emphasize that the current paper focuses on introducing the test and its theoretical framework.

### Strengths and Weaknesses
Strengths:
- The paper addresses a significant philosophical question regarding self-awareness in LLMs, which is often overlooked in rigorous academic discourse.
- It is well-written, demonstrating a strong command of the philosophy of mind and providing a clear definition of self-awareness.
- The paper provides a clear structure for the self-awareness test, emphasizing its objective nature and the necessity for subjects to identify their own outputs.
- The authors effectively address the distinction between general and specific test instances, highlighting the test's comprehensive applicability.

Weaknesses:
- The proposed methodology for assessing self-awareness is flawed, as identifying oneself in a dialogue does not equate to true self-awareness.
- The distinction between self-recognition and self-awareness is not adequately addressed, leading to potential misinterpretations of the results.
- The writing style lacks the academic rigor expected for a NeurIPS paper, with informal expressions and unclear metaphors.
- The distinction between the general and particular tests lacks clarity, which may confuse readers.
- The argument that self-awareness can be reduced to a supervised decision problem is contested, suggesting a need for a more robust philosophical grounding.

### Suggestions for Improvement
We recommend that the authors improve the clarity of the distinction between self-awareness and self-recognition, ensuring that the test accurately reflects the philosophical underpinnings of self-awareness. Additionally, we suggest that the authors clarify the distinction between the general and particular tests, potentially by focusing on one version due to space constraints. The authors should also provide a more rigorous analysis of the limitations of their test, particularly regarding the implications of memory and context on self-awareness. Furthermore, we advise strengthening their philosophical arguments against the reduction of self-awareness to a supervised decision problem, ensuring that the essence of self-awareness is thoroughly articulated. Lastly, we recommend revising the writing style to align with academic standards, eliminating informal expressions and ensuring precise language throughout the paper.
