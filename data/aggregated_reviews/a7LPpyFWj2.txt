Aggregated Review:
### Key Points
This paper presents DECO-Bench, a unified benchmark framework for evaluating decoupled task-agnostic synthetic data release methods in privacy-preserving machine learning. The framework integrates various decoupling techniques with synthetic data generation and evaluation protocols, including Cross-Entropy Decoupling (CE), Cross-Entropy with Masking (CE + Mask), and Metric Learning. The evaluation employs Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) to generate decoupled datasets, assessing privacy-utility trade-offs. The authors conduct extensive experiments, particularly focusing on image classification tasks, and release their source code, pre-trained models, and datasets to facilitate further research.

### Strengths and Weaknesses
Strengths:  
1. DECO-Bench's task and data agnostic design enhances its utility across various domains and data types.  
2. The framework provides a comprehensive evaluation of both privacy and data utility, ensuring practical applicability while maintaining privacy.  
3. The experimental setup is meticulously crafted, allowing for a deep understanding of the performance and generalizability of different decoupling techniques.  

Weaknesses:  
1. The framework lacks formal privacy guarantees, relying on empirical evaluations without clarifying methods like differential privacy.  
2. Significant performance degradation occurs when applying the framework across different datasets, indicating limitations in generalizability.  
3. Dependency on pre-trained models may lead to information leakage and suboptimal privacy protection, as these models might not align with the target dataset.

### Suggestions for Improvement
We recommend that the authors improve the paper by including formal privacy guarantees to strengthen its contribution. Additionally, addressing the limitations in cross-dataset generalization through more extensive evaluations on diverse datasets would enhance the framework's robustness. Clarifying the implementation details of specific decoupling techniques and their integration with the image generation pipeline would also improve clarity. Finally, an analysis of computational complexity and potential optimization strategies should be included to address concerns about computational overhead.
