Aggregated Review:
### Key Points
This paper presents FABRIC, a novel model for multi-document summarization that employs simplicial complex layers and sheaf graph attention to capture complex relationships among documents. The authors demonstrate that FABRIC consistently outperforms 11 baseline models across four popular multi-document summarization datasets, supported by both automatic metrics and human evaluations.

### Strengths and Weaknesses
Strengths:
- The paper introduces a unique approach using simplicial complexes and sheaf graphs, enhancing the understanding of interconnections among multiple documents.
- It shows superior performance in multi-document summarization tasks compared to existing models.
- The experimental setup is robust, and the paper is well-organized.

Weaknesses:
- Claims regarding improvements in faithfulness and factuality are not adequately assessed, as automatic metrics and human evaluations do not address these aspects.
- The methodology, particularly the graph construction and the equations, lacks clarity and requires further explanation.
- The human evaluation process raises questions about inter-annotator agreement and the criteria used for assessment.

### Suggestions for Improvement
We recommend that the authors improve the clarity of the methodology, particularly by providing more background on simplicial complexes and sheaf graphs, and by explaining the rationale for their use over traditional graphs. Additionally, the authors should conduct an ablation study to clarify the contributions of each module. It is essential to address the human evaluation concerns by providing details on inter-annotator agreement and the criteria used for evaluations. Lastly, we suggest that the authors consider using datasets with a higher average number of documents per sample to further validate the effectiveness of their proposed method.
