Aggregated Review:
### Key Points
This paper presents a study on event extraction in email threads, focusing on event trigger identification, classification, argument identification, and classification. The authors define a taxonomy of event types and argument types, applying it to annotate 1.5K email threads from the Enron dataset. They evaluate sequence labeling, sequence-to-sequence, and few-shot generative approaches, finding that sequence labeling models perform best in this closed domain. The authors also highlight challenges such as identifying non-contiguous triggers and non-named entity arguments.

### Strengths and Weaknesses
Strengths:
- The paper contributes significantly to the information extraction field by defining a taxonomy for event extraction in corporate email threads and releasing the MailEx dataset, which is valuable for evaluating information extraction models.
- The dataset addresses critical characteristics of email data and provides a comprehensive analysis of unique challenges in email event extraction.
- The authors adequately describe the experimental settings and perform qualitative analyses, enhancing reproducibility.

Weaknesses:
- The granularity of the proposed event types is limited, primarily focusing on specific business tasks, which may not encompass the broader range of events typically found in emails.
- Some sections, such as the discussion on the end-to-end extraction model, lack clarity and could benefit from improved language for better comprehension.
- The criteria for designing the event type schema are not well justified, leading to potential ambiguities in argument identification due to non-continuous spans.

### Suggestions for Improvement
We recommend that the authors improve the clarity of the language in sections discussing the end-to-end extraction model to enhance comprehension. Additionally, the authors should consider extending the event type set to include a broader range of event types found in other datasets. We also suggest providing statistical test results to support model comparison claims and clarifying the criteria used for designing the taxonomy of event types. Furthermore, addressing the challenges related to non-continuous argument spans would strengthen the paper.
