Aggregated Review:
### Key Points
This paper presents an exploration of transductive learning, focusing on the compactness of transductive sample complexity. The authors demonstrate that for a broad class of loss functions, a hypothesis class can be learned with a specific transductive sample complexity if and only if all its finite projections can be learned with the same sample complexity. This finding applies to both realizable and agnostic learning settings. The authors also connect their results to PAC learning, revealing an almost exact form of compactness for realizable PAC learning.

### Strengths and Weaknesses
Strengths:
- The paper offers a rigorous analysis of compactness in transductive learning, presenting an "exact" compactness result that avoids asymptotic limitations.
- It introduces a novel framework by generalizing classic marriage theorems for bipartite graphs, establishing a connection between the learnability of a hypothesis class and its finite projections.
- The writing is clear, with well-defined concepts and logical structure.

Weaknesses:
- The importance of the transductive learning model is not convincingly articulated, lacking a discussion on its relevance compared to classical PAC learning.
- The paper does not explore practical implications or applications of the compactness results, nor does it include empirical validation.
- A more thorough comparison to existing work on compactness in learning theory is needed to clarify the novelty of the contribution.

### Suggestions for Improvement
We recommend that the authors improve the discussion on the importance of the transductive learning model, explaining its unique contributions compared to classical PAC learning. Additionally, the authors should explore potential applications of their compactness results in real-world scenarios and consider including empirical studies to validate their theoretical findings. Strengthening the comparison to existing work on compactness in learning theory would also enhance the paper's impact. Finally, a dedicated section discussing the limitations of their assumptions, particularly regarding proper versus improper learners, would be beneficial.
